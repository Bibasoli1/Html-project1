For the past few years, January 1 is unofficially celebrated as the Nepali Topi Diwas (Nepali Cap Day). Every year, most Nepalis are seen coming out flaunting either ‘Dhaka’ topi or ‘Bhadgaule’ topi. Here are some Famous Topi in Nepal.

Dhaka Topi

It became popular during the reign of King Mahendra, who ruled between 1955 and 1972, and made wearing a Dhaka topi mandatory for official photographs for passports and documents.[5] Dhaka Topis are given away as gifts during Dashain and Tihar festivals

Bhadgaunle Topi

A Bhadgaunle Topi (Nepali: भादगाउँले टोपी, romanized: Bhādagāũle Ṭopī) or Kalo Topi (Nepali: कालो टोपी) is a type of hat worn by Nepalis and Indian Gorkhas.[1] The hat has been adopted as a part of Nepali national dress for men, as an alternative to the Palpali Dhaka topi.

Shyamung Topi

Shyamu or shyamung is a typically tall, cylindrical hat worn by both Sherpa men and women, but majorly by women. The brim of this Nepali topi is made out of wool and is usually parted facing upwards or the crown of the hat

Types of Hats & It's Cultural Importance

Other types of Hat rather than previous one.

Paga Topi

Paga is traditional headgear for Limbu men. It is a type of cap that is pointy and is tied at the back with long strips.
Paga is usually white or cream in colour and is very different from regular Dhaka topi. Besides this Nepali topi, many Limbu men also flaunt Dhaka topi with their traditional attire.

Shyamu Topi

Shyamu or shyamung is a typically tall, cylindrical hat worn by both Sherpa men and women, but majorly by women.
The brim of this Nepali topi is made out of wool and is usually parted facing upwards or the crown of the hat. The crown of the hat is generally colourful. Apart from this, Sherpa men also wear fiwgsya or thangsya, which basically is a hat with a stiff brim that is slightly curved on the sides.

Paag Topi

Paag is the traditional Nepali topi of Maithali people living in Nepal as well as India. It is a symbol of pride and honour in the Mithila region.
Paag comes in different colours including red, mustard, and white. The colours carry a lot of meanings. For example, a red paag is generally worn by the bridegrooms or boys during their bratabandha whereas elder people wear white paags.
